SECTION ONE: CUSTODY AND WALLET INFRASTRUCTURE

Let's start with the custody infrastructure news, which was dominated by security incidents this week.

The biggest story is the StablR exploit. An attacker exploited a compromised multisig signing key to mint 13.5 million dollars worth of unbacked E U R R and U S D R stablecoins. When they dumped those tokens on decentralized exchange pools, both coins depegged hard — E U R R fell to 85 cents and U S D R dropped as low as 40 cents. Total losses are estimated between 2.8 and 10 million dollars depending on how you measure it.

The minting-key attack vector here is a direct indictment of hot-wallet multisig for stablecoin issuance. If your platform has any signing authority in minting pipelines — or if you custody assets for stablecoin issuers — this should be a design review trigger. M P C with hardware security module-enforced policy rules needs to be non-negotiable in those workflows.

Also in key management news this week: Polymarket, the prediction market platform that just partnered with Nasdaq on private company valuation markets, lost roughly 700 thousand dollars in a private key hack. As prediction markets become more institutional, they will eventually need institutional-grade key management. This is an early signal of a future custody opportunity — and a warning about operating adjacent to platforms with weak key hygiene.

The third security incident this week is the rogue Safe module exploit. A third-party module called SquidRouterModule was exploited for 3.2 million dollars from Safe smart contract wallets. Both Squid and Safe Labs disclaimed responsibility, saying neither of them deployed this module. That disclaimer is almost more troubling than the exploit itself — if neither protocol owner knows who put this module in place, it means there's a supply chain governance gap in the smart wallet ecosystem. If your platform supports Safe-based custody, you need an explicit module allowlist policy and an audit trail for every third-party module attached to client wallets.

On a more positive note from the custody world: UAE-backed A D I Chain integrated with Ledger's self-custody platform, extending hardware wallet support to A D I token holders as the network expands its stablecoin and tokenized asset footprint across the Gulf. The Gulf region is building serious blockchain infrastructure, and sovereign-adjacent chains are choosing custody partners now. Track which platforms are winning integration deals there — that shapes what institutional clients in the U A E will expect to connect to.

SECTION TWO: STABLECOINS AND PAYMENTS

Three notable stablecoin and payments stories this week.

First: Tether and the government of Georgia announced GELT, a stablecoin pegged to the Georgian lari. Georgia released stablecoin rules in March 2026 covering reserve backing, issuer documentation, and external audit requirements, and GELT is explicitly designed to align with emerging U.S. stablecoin regulations as well. Tether's playbook — launch government-backed local-currency stablecoins in jurisdictions with clear reserve rules — is clearly accelerating. This is the kind of stablecoin your treasury management clients will want to hold and settle in. Make sure your platform can support non-dollar stablecoin assets and the compliance documentation that these local regulatory regimes require.

Second: E C B President Christine Lagarde formally warned E U finance ministers that easing M i C A's restrictions on euro-denominated stablecoins would destabilize bank funding and weaken interest rate policy transmission. This is significant. The E C B is actively lobbying against euro stablecoin growth. For your European clients, this means structural headwinds for using stablecoins in treasury operations will persist. Plan for U S D C and dollar-stablecoin-first workflows in E U market segments, and keep an eye on whether M i C A amendment proposals pass against E C B objections.

Third: A House subcommittee hearing on B S A — the Bank Secrecy Act — exposed a significant divide over modernizing the 1970-era A M L framework. Crypto and traditional bank representatives both argued it imposes disproportionate compliance costs, even as the Trump administration has been expanding its reach. B S A reform would directly affect K Y C and A M L obligations for custody and payment platforms. Even incremental changes to transaction reporting thresholds or travel rule scope will have product compliance implications. This is worth a memo to your legal team now.

SECTION FOUR: REGULATION AND POLICY

The regulatory story this week centered entirely on the S E C and tokenized securities.

The S E C delayed its anticipated innovation exemption for tokenized N M S stocks, citing concerns about third-party tokens — instruments that offer stock exposure without being issuer-sponsored — potentially falling under the exemption. This came alongside multiple clarifying statements from Commissioner Peirce, who pushed back on what she called "hyperbole" surrounding the proposed exemption. Her message was clear: the exemption would cover only digital representations of existing equities issued by the company itself. Not synthetics. Not third-party instruments that reference stock prices.

For custody platforms, the strategic read here is straightforward. The S E C is ultimately moving toward a framework that permits issuer-sponsored tokenized equities under a structured exemption. The delay is about scope management, not reversal. Build your compliance review checklist for evaluating tokenized equity issuances against the issuer-sponsorship criteria now, before the exemption text is finalized.

SECTION FIVE: SMART CONTRACTS AND INFRASTRUCTURE

Three stories from the infrastructure layer this week.

LayerZero published its detailed post-mortem on the 292 million dollar Kelp exploit from May 18. The incident report traces the breach to DPRK-linked group TraderTraitor, which conducted a six-week reconnaissance campaign before striking. Critically, Kelp had downgraded its D V N configuration — that's Decentralized Verification Network — from 2-of-2 to 1-of-1 roughly six weeks before the exploit. LayerZero says it approved that configuration change. In response, LayerZero has now set 3-of-3 D V N as the protocol default.

The takeaway for custody platforms: DPRK-affiliated attackers are conducting multi-week campaigns before striking bridge infrastructure. If your clients use cross-chain bridging for any asset movements, this incident is a direct argument for requiring bridge provider security audits and minimum D V N threshold policies as part of your vendor due diligence framework.

OpenZeppelin published a compelling blog post this week arguing that traditional vendor procurement frameworks — I S O 27001, S O C 2, domicile checks — are insufficient for evaluating blockchain infrastructure vendors. As traditional finance moves on-chain, they argue, procurement processes need crypto-native additions: on-chain audit trails, smart contract coverage terms, and protocol-level risk assessments. This is directly applicable to how your enterprise clients evaluate your platform. Consider publishing a custody vendor due diligence guide that helps institutional clients ask the right crypto-native security questions. It positions you as the standard-setter and makes your own compliance credentials more legible to TradFi procurement teams.
